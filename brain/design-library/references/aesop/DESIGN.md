## 1. Visual Theme & Atmosphere

Aesop is the benchmark for luxury achieved through restraint. Where most luxury brands rely on serif typography, gilded accents, or dramatic contrast, Aesop proves that a single sans-serif typeface family, an earth-toned palette, and disciplined whitespace can communicate more luxury than any ornament. The digital presence is an extension of the physical retail experience -- quiet, considered, and deeply respectful of the viewer's attention.

The palette is built on botanical earth tones -- a warm parchment ground (`#F6F5E8`) that recalls aged linen and apothecary paper, paired with forest green-blacks (`#333D2C`) and clay neutrals (`#696158`) that reference the brand's botanical ingredients. There is no electric accent color, no gradient, no vibrancy. The entire color world operates in the muted register of dried herbs, amber glass bottles, and stone pharmacy counters.

Typography is the singular luxury instrument. **Suisse Intl** (by Swiss Typefaces, designed by Ian Party) carries the entire digital system -- navigation, headings, body, buttons, labels, product names, captions. This is Aesop's most radical design decision: one typeface family at luxury level, where most competitors would use 2-3 faces. The hierarchy is created entirely through weight modulation (300 Light for display, 400 Regular for body, 500 Medium for labels/emphasis), size scaling, and letter-spacing -- never through typeface contrast. A secondary face, **Zapf Humanist 601** (Bitstream's Optima), appears only in editorial/storytelling moments (The Fabulist literary magazine, ingredient stories) as a humanist counterpoint. **Neue Helvetica** is reserved for physical packaging only and does not appear on the website.

The logo typeface is **Optima** -- a humanist sans-serif with subtle flared stroke terminals that give it a calligraphic warmth unusual for a sans-serif. The macron over the "e" (Aesop) is the single decorative element in the entire brand identity, referencing classical Greek typography. This one diacritical mark does more work than most brands' entire decorative systems.

Surfaces are completely flat. There are no layered shadows, no glassmorphism, no blur effects. The only depth cue is the warm-cool shift between surface tones (#F6F5E8 parchment vs #FAF9F4 warm white vs #FFFEF2 cream). Cards have zero border-radius. Buttons have zero border-radius. Inputs use underline-only styling (bottom border). This absolute commitment to the rectangle is a conscious design philosophy: the product bottles are the only curves in the Aesop world.

Motion is minimal and deliberate. Transitions use slow, organic easing -- typically 400-600ms durations with ease-out curves. Page transitions feel like turning pages in a well-made book. Navigation reveals products through elegant slide-and-fade choreography. There is never a bounce, spring, or playful overshoot. Every motion communicates "we have nothing to rush."

**Key Characteristics:**
- Warm parchment-linen backgrounds (`#F6F5E8`, `#FAF9F4`) -- never pure white, never cool gray
- Near-black text (`#252525`) rather than pure black -- softer on the eye, more refined
- Single typeface system (Suisse Intl) creating hierarchy through weight/size/spacing alone
- Optima (Zapf Humanist) reserved exclusively for editorial/literary moments
- Zero border-radius on all UI components -- buttons, cards, inputs, badges
- Underline-only form inputs (bottom border, no box)
- Product photography on neutral warm grounds, products shot as art objects
- Earth-tone color world: forest, olive, bark, clay, stone, sand, parchment, cream
- Slow, organic motion (400-600ms, ease-out) -- no spring physics, no bouncing
- Editorial content (The Fabulist) treated as a first-class brand pillar, not marketing

## 2. Color Palette & Roles

### Primary

- **Aesop Forest** (`#333D2C`): The signature brand color. A deep, muted green-black that references botanical ingredients and apothecary tradition. Used for primary badges, accent moments, and brand-level emphasis. Never used as a large background fill on the web -- it lives primarily on packaging (the iconic amber bottle label background).

### Brand & Dark

- **Near Black** (`#252525`): The dominant text and UI color. Deliberately not pure black -- the slight warmth makes long-form reading more comfortable and aligns with the organic palette. Used for all body text, headings, button fills, borders.
- **Cream** (`#FFFEF2`): The lightest surface. Used for button text on dark backgrounds, overlay text, and the purest highlight moments.

### Neutral Scale (Earth Progression)

- **Olive** (`#4A5240`): Secondary dark tone for hover states and emphasis.
- **Bark** (`#544D4B`): Dark warm neutral, used for secondary text, subtle borders.
- **Clay** (`#69615F`): Muted text, form labels, placeholder text, secondary information.
- **Stone** (`#8C857E`): Disabled states, divider lines, tertiary UI elements.
- **Sand** (`#C5BBAE`): Light borders, subtle separators, background accents.
- **Parchment** (`#F6F5E8`): Primary surface background. The signature warm linen ground.
- **Warm White** (`#FAF9F4`): Navigation background, card surfaces, elevated content areas.

### Accent

- **Amber** (`#D4A76A`): Used with extreme restraint for seasonal/gifting moments. References the iconic amber glass of Aesop bottles. Never appears as a CTA or UI accent in the standard experience.

### Semantic

- **Error** (`#9B2C2C`): Deep burgundy-red, muted to avoid disrupting the earth-tone palette.
- **Success** (`#2D5016`): Dark forest green, harmonizes with the brand's botanical palette.

### Color Philosophy

The Aesop color strategy is subtractive, not additive. Where most brands ask "what colors should we add?", Aesop asks "what colors can we remove?" The result is a palette that functions like a painter's limited earth-tone palette -- every color relates to the physical world of plants, soil, stone, and aged paper. No synthetic color appears anywhere in the system.

### Typography Philosophy: Luxury Through Restraint

Aesop's type system demonstrates that you do not need a serif to communicate luxury. The key decisions:

1. **Single-family hierarchy**: Suisse Intl at weights 300/400/500 covers the entire UI. No display face, no decorative face, no script. This mirrors the brand's product philosophy: do fewer things, do them impeccably.

2. **Light weight for display**: Display headings at weight 300 (Light) is the signature move. Where most brands go bold for impact, Aesop goes light -- the thin strokes create breathing room and communicate confidence. The brand does not need to shout.

3. **Tight negative letter-spacing on display**: -0.02em at hero sizes tightens the word shapes, creating a more architectural, block-like quality. This is a key editorial technique borrowed from print magazine design.

4. **Generous line-height on body**: 1.6 line-height on body text creates exceptional readability and a sense of spaciousness. This is "bookish" line-height, typical of well-set literary texts.

5. **Uppercase + wide tracking for labels**: Labels use 500 weight, uppercase, and 0.06-0.08em tracking. This creates a quiet authority without adding visual weight.

6. **Optima for editorial warmth**: The occasional use of Zapf Humanist (Optima) in editorial contexts introduces a subtle humanist warmth -- the flared stroke terminals of Optima reference calligraphy without being a serif. This is the typographic equivalent of a handwritten note inside a precisely machined box.

- **Desktop**: 12-column grid, max-width 1440px, 24px gutters, 80px side margins
- **Tablet**: 8-column grid, 20px gutters, 40px side margins
- **Mobile**: 4-column grid, 16px gutters, 20px side margins
- **Product grid**: 4 columns desktop, 3 columns tablet, 2 columns mobile
- **Editorial grid**: Asymmetric layouts common -- 7:5 or 8:4 splits for text/image pairs

### Layout Patterns

1. **Full-bleed hero**: Full-viewport-width image with overlaid text, text positioned in the lower third (cinematographic composition). Used for campaign launches and seasonal stories.

2. **Split editorial**: 50/50 or 60/40 text-image splits with generous padding. Text side always has more breathing room than feels necessary. This "excess" whitespace is intentional -- it signals unhurried luxury.

3. **Product grid**: Clean 4-column grid with consistent vertical rhythm. Product images float on warm neutral backgrounds. Hover reveals minimal additional information (no dramatic card transforms).

4. **Ingredient storytelling**: Single-column, narrow measure (60ch max), generous margins. Long-form text set in body-large (16px) at 1.6 line-height. Pull quotes in Optima. This mirrors the layout of a well-designed book page.

5. **The Fabulist (literary)**: Manuscript-inspired layout. Deliberately austere -- as if reading an unadorned proof of a literary work. Minimal decoration, maximum focus on text.

## 5. Motion & Animation

### Timing

| Token | Duration | Easing | Usage |
|-------|----------|--------|-------|
| instant | 100ms | ease-out | Hover color changes, opacity toggles |
| fast | 200ms | ease-out | Button state changes, input focus |

| slow | 600ms | cubic-bezier(0.25, 0.1, 0.25, 1) | Navigation panels, overlays, page transitions |
| deliberate | 800ms | cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) | Hero image reveals, full-page transitions |

### Motion Philosophy

Aesop's motion language is borrowed from physical retail: the slow, considered way a consultant opens a drawer, presents a product, or pours a sample. Every animation should feel like it has mass and intentionality. Key principles:

1. **No spring physics**: Everything uses curve-based easing, never bounce or overshoot. Springs feel playful; Aesop is not playful.

2. **No parallax**: Background elements do not move at different speeds. The page scrolls as a single surface, like turning pages in a book.

3. **Opacity over transform**: Elements prefer to fade in rather than slide in. When movement is used, it is subtle (8-16px translate, never more).

4. **Staggered reveals**: Grid items reveal in sequence with 50-80ms stagger, creating a gentle cascade effect rather than all-at-once appearance.

5. **Navigation choreography**: The mega-menu uses a coordinated slide-down + fade-in of content, with product images animating in sequence. This is the most complex motion on the site.

6. **Product image hover**: Subtle opacity shift (0.95 to 1.0) or slight scale (1.0 to 1.02 over 400ms). Never a dramatic zoom or elevation change.

## 7. Product-as-Art-Object Presentation

The single most distinctive pattern in Aesop's digital design is how products are photographed and presented:

1. **Neutral ground**: Products are photographed on warm, desaturated backgrounds that complement the bottle/tube color. Never on pure white. The background colors are pulled from the brand palette (parchment, sand, cream).

2. **Single product hero**: Product detail pages feature a single bottle photographed at slightly above eye level, with visible label typography. The bottle IS the hero -- no lifestyle context, no hands, no application shots.

3. **Shadow and light**: Photography uses soft, directional lighting that creates gentle shadows, giving the bottles a three-dimensional presence. The lighting references museum display or gallery photography.

4. **Ingredient botanical**: Product pages feature ingredient imagery -- close-up photography of botanicals (leaves, seeds, bark) shot in the same warm-neutral palette as the product shots. This creates a visual narrative: plant to product.

5. **Scale and proportion**: Products are often photographed at 2-3x actual size, making the amber glass and label typography legible and sculptural. The bottle becomes an architectural object.

## 9. Protocolo Luxo -- Design Lessons for REIS [IA]

### What Aesop teaches about luxury-through-typography:

1. **One typeface is enough if it is the right typeface.** Suisse Intl proves that a well-designed neo-grotesque with multiple weights can carry an entire luxury brand. The lesson: mastery of a single instrument beats competence with three.

2. **Light weight > Bold for luxury display.** Aesop uses 300 (Light) for heroes. Light weight creates more whitespace within letterforms, making the typography feel open, confident, and unhurried. Bold shouts; Light whispers -- and the whisper carries further.

3. **Negative letter-spacing on display, positive on labels.** Tightening display type (-0.02em) and loosening label type (+0.06em) creates two distinct registers from the same typeface. This is the core editorial typography technique.

4. **The color of "not-quite-black" matters.** #252525 vs #000000 is a subtle but real difference. The slightly warm near-black is softer, more organic, more aligned with the earth-tone palette.

5. **Zero border-radius is a philosophical position.** Aesop's commitment to sharp rectangles across all UI elements says: we are precise, we are architectural, we do not soften our edges. The rounded corners belong to the product bottles alone.

6. **Editorial content is brand equity, not content marketing.** The Fabulist literary magazine, ingredient stories, and botanical photography are not conversion funnels -- they are brand-building investments that justify premium pricing.
